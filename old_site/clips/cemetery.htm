Date: Sunday, November 3, 2002

By: By KEVIN PURDY - Niagara Correspondent



Illustration: RONALD J. COLLERAN/Buffalo News
When people say Daniel Bille knows where all the bodies are buried, they're not kidding. Bille spends some of his time amid the grave markers in Sweeney Cemetery, final resting place of Asa Ransom and other famous Western New Yorkers.

NORTH TONAWANDA

As Daniel Bille continues to dig into the history of this city's cemeteries, he's finding more bodies time has forgotten and raising a lot more questions that he wants answered.

After mounting an exhaustive project to compile a complete census of the bodies buried in the city's Sweeney Cemetery nearly four years ago, Bille, along with others in the community, wants to see the final homes of some of Niagara County's most notable deceased given new life with the help of state grants.

Bille, the city's historian and a member of the recently created City Cemetery Board, believes, however, that the city first needs to answer many of the questions that date back a century or more.

When, and by whom, for instance, was the shed in Sweeney Cemetery shed built? (It originally was used as temporary storage of those who passed away in the winter months.) And who can still be buried in Sweeney? (The cemetery is nearly full, with around 1,250 bodies interred, but requests for burial information still come into the city attorney's office.)

Perhaps the most interesting question revolves around a one-acre "potter's field," whose exact owner is unknown.

"The question is: Where are the bodies buried? How many are there? The answer is: We don't have a clue," said Bille.

Bille said that once the city has complete records of its cemeteries and other burial grounds, state and historical grants, such as the $250,000 the City of Tonawanda recently received for the historic preservation of three Main Street cemeteries, may be available to North Tonawanda.

Until then, it appears that Bille may have to go "digging" again, although this time he may need help from a research team at the University at Buffalo instead of a dedicated Eagle Scout.

Sweeney Cemetery began as a private burial site on farmland owned by one of the Village of Tonawanda's first residents, Col. John Sweeney, and contains the bodies of many historic notables from both sides of the Niagara, including John Simson, who erected the first sawmill at the Tonawanda Creek dam; Asa Ransom (1801-1891), the first white child born in Erie County; and James Carney (1800-1881), a Niagara pioneer with a commanding, 15-foot memorial in the heart of the cemetery.

Sweeney was also the reburial site for many originally buried in the Tonawanda Creek Cemetery after substantial erosion of the creek's soil and the loss of some bodies into the river.

Since it was first incorporated in 1868, numerous cemetery mishaps -- a deed lost by an interim owner between 1919 and 1923, acts of vandalism in the 1950s, and the lack of a governing cemetery board since the Col. John Sweeney Rural Cemetery corporation dissolved in 1977 -- led Bille to begin the long process of compiling an exact list of who was buried in the cemetery, and where.

By utilizing burial maps dating to the 1890s, burial lists from the 1920s and other documents, Bille, along with a Boy Scout working on his Eagle Scout project and others dedicated to the project, have come up with a nearly complete record of the burials in Sweeney.

The record came in handy last July, when Tonawanda resident Sally Foster buried her grandfather Lauren Hollister there, but officials believe there may be more deeds to plots in the cemetery than can be filled.

After two of her aunts provided the city attorney's office with a deed to a plot dating back to the 1930s and purchased for $135, a location was found for Hollister -- a World War II rifleman said to have fought in the first unit to enter a German concentration camp -- without having to exhume any bodies.

Foster said she and her family had considered burying another relative in the remaining plot allotted to her family but was unsure of how much space was available for burial.

"It depends on what's available, but we're not really sure," said Foster. "We might be able to put someone else there, if they're cremated, but we don't really know."

Linda Krempa, a legal stenographer in the city attorney's office and member of the Cemetery Board, said that Foster's situation was a common one.

"We have lots of inquiries here, but there's not a lot we can do unless they can provide a deed," said Krempa. "It looks like there's a lot of room over there, but there's not."

Rohr Street operated by St. Paul's Lutheran Church held no deed at their burial site, where the unknown deceased of North Tonawanda were laid to rest in a "potter's field."

According to Velma Camann, assistant superintendent of St. Paul's cemeteries, the church has no records of the graveyard's existence other than the two grave markers at the site. Bille said he believes St. Paul's deed for the property may have been lost.

Bille said there was no way of telling how many bodies could be buried in the field without the help of a ground penetrating radar device, and has contacted the University at Buffalo's departments of archaeology and geology to discuss a possible collaboration.

Thomas Jordan, a doctoral candidate in UB's geology department, has worked with such radar at grave sites in Lewiston and Clarence, as well as helping map subterranean tunnels in Eden some believe may have been used as part of the Underground Railroad used to help runaway slaves reach freedom before the Civil War.

Jordan said that the radar device, a two-antennae system that can often be run by foot or truck, is especially useful to graveyard researchers, as it can detect soil disturbances that can indicated the burial of even a small infant.

"A pine box may not be present with some older burials, but we can still see if there was somebody there at some point," said Jordan.

Under state law, villages and towns are responsible for maintaining any grave sites of unknowns or burial grounds abandoned by their independent operators. But cities such as North Tonawanda are exempted.

Still, Bille believes that many of the burials may date back as far as the Revolutionary War, or could possibly be casualties from among the sailors who passed through town during the region's lumber and shipping boom, and says the site should be documented.

At an Oct. 15 Common Council meeting, the Council approved Mayor David Burgio's proposal for a cemetery board, in order to pursue "expanded New York State funding" for restoring historic cemeteries, hoping to attract funds like those secured for the City of Tonawanda by Assemblyman Robin L. Schimminger, D-Town of Tonawanda, to restore some of Sweeney's damaged headstones and build the site into one suitable for historical tour groups.

"We're going on (Bille's) say-so on what's available to a city with a board," said Burgio. "Sweeney is in need of repair, and we'd like to get that done somehow."

Richard D. Fishman, director of the state division of cemeteries, said funds from the state cemetery board were generally available only to municipalities that had recently taken over an abandoned cemetery or had large monuments in dire need of repair.

North Tonawanda, Fishman said, could receive grants from general and historic state and national funds if its cemeteries were used as historic landmarks.

"We are beginning to see an increase in the number of towns and cities looking into (historic) grants," said Fishman. "Many of the cemetery boards are aging, and the money that was there before is not anymore."

Schimminger said that attempts to integrate a cemetery into an overall tourism and beautification focus must take into account their principal purpose: to pay respect to community members who have died.

"Whatever is done should be done with great care and consideration. Not everybody likes the idea of Forest Lawn, for example," Schimminger said, referring to Buffalo's historic cemetery in which period re-enactments and other activities are held.

City of Tonawanda Mayor Jack E. Gallagher said he intends to keep the Main Street cemeteries a "low-key" draw to the community.

"People sometimes cringe when you say something might become a 'tourist attraction.' I see it as more of a tourist destination, where people can come and pay respect," he said.

A small storage and groundskeeper's building in Sweeney cemetery could be one historical point of reference, Bille and others say, for the architectural features of its roof and old-fashioned design. But it appears on none of Bille's or the city's maps, and no records exist of its construction or usage.

For Bille, the problem is just one more question whose only answer might have been buried along with the cemetery's inhabitants.
